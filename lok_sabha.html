About Lok Sabha Elections

The Lok Sabha is the lower house of India's Parliament, composed of elected representatives. It plays a vital role in the country's legislative process, including making laws, approving budgets, and overseeing the government. Members of the Lok Sabha are elected through general elections held every five years, representing various constituencies across India.

The Lok Sabha holds significant power, including the ability to form governments and enact legislation. It debates and discusses important national issues, reflecting the diverse interests and concerns of India's population. Through its proceedings, the Lok Sabha contributes to shaping the country's policies and direction.

As the primary legislative body in India, the Lok Sabha plays a crucial role in the functioning of the country's democracy.

Political Parties

The BSP states that it represents the people at the lowest levels of the Hindu social system—those officially designated as members of the Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, and Other Backward Classes—as well as other religious and social minorities.

Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP), pro-Hindu political party of postindependence India. The party has enjoyed broad support among members of the higher castes and in northern India. It has attempted to attract support from lower castes, particularly through the appointment of several lower-caste members to prominent party positions.

The CPI’s initial objectives combined militant anti-imperialist patriotism with internationalism to create a movement parallel to the nonviolent civil disobedience (satyagraha) campaigns led by Mohandas K. Gandhi and the Indian National Congress (Congress Party).

Indian National Congress, broadly based political party of India. Formed in 1885, the Indian National Congress dominated the Indian movement for independence from Great Britain. It subsequently formed most of India’s governments from the time of independence and often had a strong presence in many state governments.
